Themes and traits

The theme of social justice and equality shows up in several events and people. Ida B. Wells and Mary Eliza Church Terrell worked hard to fight against racism and sexism. The 19th Amendment was a big win for women’s right to vote and gender equality. The Civil Rights Movement was an important time in the fight for racial justice, showing how powerful people can be when they work together. On the other hand, Japanese American internment during World War II was a clear example of civil rights being violated, reminding us to always protect people’s freedoms.

The theme of war and conflict shows up in many important events. The Battle of Little Big Horn and the Civil War were key moments in American history. The Battle of Somme showed the heavy losses of World War I. The Pearl Harbor attack pulled the U.S. into World War II. Cold War tensions and McCarthyism revealed the fears and clashes of ideas in the 20th century. 9/11 and the War on Terror show how modern conflicts shape the world today. These events remind us how wars can change nations and people, bringing big political, social, and cultural changes.

Advocacy for social change is a common trait. Ida B. Wells and Mary Eliza Church Terrell fought for civil rights and racial equality. The 19th Amendment, which gave women the right to vote, connects to their work for gender equality. The Civil Rights Movement carried on their fight for justice. This shows a strong trait of standing up for justice, equality, and making society better.

War and its consequences is an important trait. The Battle of Little Big Horn, the Civil War, and the Battle of Somme were major conflicts that showed the human and political costs of war. The Pearl Harbor attack and 9/11 brought the U.S. into big global conflicts. Cold War tensions and McCarthyism showed how ideas can lead to conflict without fighting. This theme highlights how war affects both the battlefield and societies as a whole.

Transformative economic and social shifts is another common trait. The Great Depression and the Dust Bowl showed the struggles of the 1930s with economic and environmental challenges. The rise of suburban America reflected the prosperity and changes after World War II. Social media and the internet show how technology creates new ways of living and working. This theme focuses on how events change economies, societies, and everyday life.

Related Events

The Civil War, Ida B. Wells, and The Civil Rights Movement are deeply intertwined as milestones in the ongoing struggle for equality in the United States. The Civil War (1861–1865) was a pivotal moment, fought primarily over the issue of slavery, and its aftermath led to the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, which aimed to secure freedom and rights for African Americans (History.com, 2020). However, even with the formal abolition of slavery, Black Americans continued to face systemic racism and violence, a reality that figures like Ida B. Wells tirelessly fought against. As an investigative journalist and activist, Wells brought attention to the lynching of Black Americans, working to expose and dismantle the structures of white supremacy that persisted after Reconstruction (Britannica.com, 2020). This fight for equality laid the groundwork for the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 60s, which sought to end segregation, secure voting rights, and challenge discriminatory laws (History.com, 2020). Leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and organizations like the NAACP built upon the foundations laid by activists like Wells, pushing for a more inclusive interpretation of the promises made during the Civil War. The movement culminated in landmark achievements such as the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965, but it also highlighted the ongoing struggle for full racial justice, a theme that continued well beyond these legislative victories.

Events I would teach

The Civil War (1861–1865)

The Civil War is foundational to understanding the United States' evolution. It was a turning point in the nation's history, deeply influencing its social, political, and economic structures. The war not only ended slavery but also set the stage for future civil rights struggles, making it essential for understanding systemic racism, the development of federal power, and the ongoing quest for equality. Studying the Civil War provides students with critical insights into how conflict can reshape a nation’s identity and how historical struggles for freedom continue to influence present-day discussions about race and justice.

The Civil Rights Movement (1950s–1960s)

The Civil Rights Movement is pivotal in shaping modern American society. The efforts of activists like Martin Luther King Jr., Rosa Parks, and Ida B. Wells (whose work contributed to the movement’s foundation) created significant social and legal changes, such as the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965. This movement highlights the power of nonviolent protest, grassroots organizing, and the fight against systemic oppression. It remains relevant in the ongoing struggle for racial justice and equality, making it crucial for students to understand its historical context and continuing legacy.

9/11 and the War on Terror (2001–present)

The September 11, 2001 attacks and the subsequent War on Terror changed the course of American and global history in profound ways. The attacks led to significant shifts in U.S. foreign and domestic policy, including the wars in Afghanistan and Iraq, the creation of the Department of Homeland Security, and the erosion of civil liberties in the name of national security. This event has shaped the way Americans view security, privacy, and their role in global politics. Understanding 9/11 is essential not only for grasping modern geopolitical conflicts but also for reflecting on how traumatic events can change national identities and global relations.
