The Great Depression (1929-1939)

The Great Depression began with the stock market crash of October 1929, also known as "Black Tuesday." Millions of Americans lost their jobs as businesses closed and banks failed. Unemployment reached nearly 25%, and many families struggled to find food and shelter. President Franklin D. Roosevelt responded with the New Deal, a series of programs aimed at providing relief, recovery, and reform. These programs included Social Security, public works projects, and financial regulations. Despite the efforts, the U.S. economy didn't fully recover until the onset of World War II, which created jobs through military production.

This interested me because I lived through the recession that took place 2007-2009 (The Great Recession). it wasn't as severe as the Great Depression; it was the most serious financial crisis since then. The recession was triggered by the collapse of the housing market, risky lending practices, and the failure of major financial institutions. Fortunately, I did not lose my house, but I did see others lose theirs.

The Dust Bowl (1930s)

During the 1930s, severe droughts and poor farming practices led to massive dust storms across the Great Plains, known as the Dust Bowl. The storms destroyed crops and livestock, leaving thousands of farmers without a livelihood. Many families, known as "Okies," migrated west to California in search of work and better living conditions. The environmental disaster deepened the effects of the Great Depression in rural America. Federal programs like the Soil Conservation Service were created to help farmers recover and prevent future disasters. The Dust Bowl remains one of the worst environmental crises in U.S. history.

This interested me because It shows how intertwined environmental and economic factors can compound hardships during already difficult times. This was going on while the great depression was happening, and this gives more insight into how bad it really was.

Pearl Harbor Attack

On December 7, 1941, the Japanese military launched a surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, a U.S. naval base in Hawaii. The attack destroyed or damaged eight battleships, several other vessels, and over 300 aircraft. More than 2,400 Americans were killed, with many more injured. The following day, President Franklin D. Roosevelt declared war on Japan, bringing the United States into World War II. The attack united Americans, who were previously divided about entering the war, and led to massive military mobilization. Pearl Harbor is remembered as a pivotal moment in U.S. history.

This interests me because Pearl Harbor was the first large-scale foreign attack on an American territory. I find myself comparing it to 9/11, which was the first time America was attacked on its mainland. It's crazy to think that, in all those years, the U.S. had never faced an attack on its homeland by a foreign force before 9/11, and the only previous major attempt was Pearl Harbor.

Japanese American Internment

Following the attack on Pearl Harbor, President Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, which authorized the forced relocation of Japanese Americans to internment camps. Over 120,000 people, the majority of whom were U.S. citizens, were sent to live in isolated camps for the duration of World War II. Families were forced to leave their homes, businesses, and belongings behind, often with little notice. The government justified the action as a security measure, though no evidence of espionage was found among those interned. Conditions in the camps were harsh, with overcrowding, poor sanitation, and a lack of privacy. In 1988, the U.S. government formally apologized and paid reparations to survivors.

This interests me because it highlights our fears and mistakes. We knew it was wrong, yet we still did this to people. A few bad individuals don't represent the entire group. I don’t think it was the right decision, but it can serve as an important teaching moment for many Americans. We've seen similar racism resurface when COVID-19 hit the world.
