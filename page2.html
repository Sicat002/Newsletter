19th Amendment

A strategy of passing suffrage acts in each state, nine western states adopted woman suffrage legislation by 1912. By 1916, almost all the major suffrage organizations were united behind the goal of a constitutional amendment. When New York adopted woman suffrage in 1917 and President Wilson changed his position to support an amendment in 1918, the political balance began to shift. During WWI, many women took up larger roles in society since their husbands were away fighting. The Herrin Massacre

The Herrin Massacre of June 1922 was a devastating incident born out of a coal miners' strike in Herrin, Illinois. Non-union workers, who were primarily Black and recruited from out of state without being informed they were being used as strikebreakers, were met by angry union miners who ambushed them on their way to the mine. A deadly shootout ensued, resulting in the deaths of both strikebreakers and miners. The non-union workers eventually surrendered under a promise of safe passage, but the violence only escalated. As they were marched from the mine, the crowd turned on them, killing the mine superintendent and brutally gunning down workers as they tried to escape. In total, 21 men were killed, with some hunted down in the woods or shot at a local cemetery. National disgust followed, but local juries, sympathetic to the miners, acquitted those charged, and over 200 indictments were dropped. Union president John L. Lewis sent miners back to work under the old contract, but the damage was done. The UMW's reputation was shattered, and its power continued to decline throughout the 1920s, marking the massacre as a pivotal and grim moment in labor history.

This interested me because the strikebreakers had nothing to do with this, and they were all killed and then the miners were acquitted. It’s crazy how they could shoot down people in cold blood and get away with it.

Battle of the Somme

A costly and attrition campaign that initially aimed for a rapid breakthrough to Bapaume and Cambrai, but setbacks forced British commander Sir Douglas Haig to adopt a method of limited advances to wear down German forces. Rejecting French commander Joffre's plan for a frontal assault on the Thiepval defenses, Haig concentrated attacks on the southern flank, capturing key positions but missing opportunities for further gains. The battle is notable for the first use of tanks on September 15, though their premature deployment led to limited success due to tactical mishandling and technical defects. By the time the offensive ended in November, both sides had suffered heavy casualties—over 600,000 for the Allies and more than 440,000 for the Germans—without a decisive outcome, but the tanks hinted at future warfare innovations.

Mary Church Terrell

Mary Eliza Church Terrell (1863–1954) was a pioneering American social activist, educator, and early advocate for civil rights and women's suffrage. Born to formerly enslaved parents who became successful business owners in Memphis, Tennessee, she enjoyed educational opportunities rare for African Americans at the time, earning a bachelor’s and master’s degree from Oberlin College. Terrell co-founded and served as the first president of the National Association of Colored Women, working to end racial discrimination and promote educational reform. She was also the first Black woman appointed to the District of Columbia Board of Education and a founding member of the NAACP. In 1949, she successfully fought against the exclusion of Black women from the American Association of University Women, further advancing civil rights.

This interested me because her parents were slaves and became successful, and she was the first African American woman to earn a degree.
