Mark Twain

Mark Twain was born on November 30, 1835, in the small town of Florida, MO. His real name was Samuel Langhorne Clemens. His essays and novels, including the American classic, The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, are a testament to his intelligence and insight. Twain worked as a steamboat captain for years before the Civil War. Mark Twain was a riverboat term that means two fathoms, the point at which it is safe for the boat to navigate the waters. Twain got his first big break as a writer in 1865 with an article about life in a mining camp, called Jim Smiley and His Jumping Frog, also called The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County. It was very favorably received and printed in newspapers and magazines all over the country. This led him to start becoming a travel writer and he would travel to different countries. Twain was much admired during his lifetime. The Mark Twain Prize for American Humor was created in his honor and has been given annually since 1998 to “people who have had an impact on American society in ways similar to the distinguished 19th century novelist and essayist best known as Mark Twain.”.

This interested me because America considers him to be one of the greats. Art imitates life in any form. What is happening in the present or even the past could affect art. Being able to have insight into his world and how his art came about is what interested me the most.

Ida B. Wells

Ida B. Wells was an African American journalist, abolitionist and feminist who led an anti-lynching crusade in the United States in the 1890s. Born an enslaved person in Holly Springs, Mississippi, on July 16, 1862, Wells was the oldest daughter. The Confederate states were decreed free by the Union thanks to the Emancipation Proclamation about six months after Ida's birth. At the age of 16, her parents died of a yellow fever outbreak. she convinced a nearby country school administrator that she was 18 and landed a job as a teacher. May 1884, she bought a first-class train ticket, and the crew told her to move to a car for African Americans. When she sued the train station and won, they overturned the ruling and that is what led her to becoming an activist. Mostly known for her lynching campaign which started because Three African American men Tom Moss, Calvin McDowell and Will Stewart set up a grocery store. They defended their store from white men and killed some of them. They were arrested and later taken from their cells and lynched before they could even defend themselves. This incident led her to write about more lynchings happening all over the country.

I was interested in this because in a world where men were supposed to be on “top,” she fought through it all and made a name for herself. Not only was she a woman but, an African American woman so that made it even harder for her. She created her own business and did not let anyone silence her.

The Battle of Little Big Horn

In 1875, after gold was discovered in South Dakota’s Black Hills, the U.S. Army ignored previous treaty agreements and invaded the region. By the late spring of 1876, more than 10,000 Native Americans had gathered in a camp along the Little Bighorn River. They were warned to return to their camps, or they would be attacked. The leaders of this camp were Sitting Bull and Crazy Horse. George Custer’s 7th Cavalry attacked on June 25. Custer had 600 men and were met with 3,000 native American warriors. Custer and all his men died that day. The result of this was the United States government now saw the Native Americans as a threat and confined them to their camps.

I was interested in this because the Native Americans won the battle. I do not think they took them seriously as a threat and it showed. I think two men bringing together others is inspiring. It shows with the right cause people can come together.

Civil War

Abraham Lincoln, candidate of the explicitly antislavery Republican Party, won the 1860 presidential election. During this time, 11 Southern states seceded from the Union to protect what they saw as their right to keep slaves. These states were organized as the Confederate States of America. Jefferson Davis was the Confederate president. Disregarding Lincoln’s vow, Confederate forces opened fire on Fort Sumter on April 12, 1861. This marked the beginning of the American Civil War. In September 1862 Lincoln called on the seceded states to return to the Union or have their slaves declared free. he issued the Emancipation Proclamation on January 1, 1863. It had the practical effect of permitting recruitment of African Americans for the Union army and declared all slaves in the confederate states free. The South was devastated by the war, but the Union was preserved, and the Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution, ratified in 1865, officially abolished slavery in the entire country.

This war interested me because it was between our own country. American Civil War remains the deadliest war in U.S. history. It’s crazy how the war between our own people was the deadliest we have ever had. It also is the war that ended slavery in America.
